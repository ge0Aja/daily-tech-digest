## Security & Privacy

### Google Confirms First AI-Built Zero-Day Exploit Used in Active Attack
_Google Cloud Blog_ | [Read more](https://cloud.google.com/blog/topics/threat-intelligence/ai-vulnerability-exploitation-initial-access)

Google's Threat Intelligence Group (GTIG) identified a threat actor using an LLM-generated zero-day to bypass two-factor authentication on a popular open-source web admin tool — the first confirmed in-the-wild use of AI for vulnerability discovery and exploit development. Researchers identified AI involvement through telltale artifacts: educational docstrings, a hallucinated CVSS score, and textbook-style Python output characteristic of LLM generation. GTIG coordinated disclosure with the vendor before a planned mass exploitation campaign could launch. This confirms that AI-assisted exploit development is now an active threat, not a theoretical one.

### CVE-2026-31431 "Copy Fail": Linux Kernel LPE Hits All Major Distros
_Microsoft Security Blog_ | [Read more](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/security/blog/2026/05/01/cve-2026-31431-copy-fail-vulnerability-enables-linux-root-privilege-escalation/)

A logic error in the Linux kernel's AF_ALG cryptographic module allows local unprivileged users to escalate to root via a controlled 4-byte kernel page cache write that corrupts in-memory privileged binaries without touching disk (CVSS 7.8). Virtually all distributions with kernels since 2017 are affected — Ubuntu 24.04 LTS, Amazon Linux 2023, RHEL 10.1, Debian, Fedora, and Arch Linux. Successful exploitation enables container breakout and lateral movement in Kubernetes and cloud multi-tenant environments; apply vendor kernel patches immediately or disable the AF_ALG module as an interim measure.

The EU AI Act's high-risk AI obligations apply from August 2, 2026, requiring conformity assessments, CE marking, technical documentation, and EU database registration for AI systems in biometrics, critical infrastructure, employment, credit scoring, and law enforcement. ### EU Cyber Resilience Act: Vulnerability Reporting Obligations Active September 11, 2026
_Security Boulevard_ | [Read more](https://securityboulevard.com/2026/03/the-eu-cra-treating-cybersecurity-as-product-liability/)

The EU CRA treats cybersecurity as product liability: manufacturers of digital products sold in Europe must report actively exploited vulnerabilities to ENISA and national CSIRTs within 24 hours of discovery, with reporting obligations kicking in on September 11, 2026. Only genuinely non-commercial open source is exempt — any project generating revenue (including paid support contracts) falls in scope and must maintain coordinated vulnerability disclosure workflows. This creates new legal exposure for open-core companies and individual maintainers who charge for services, fundamentally reshaping commercial software supply chain security practices.
